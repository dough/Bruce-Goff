BUILDING DESIGN 13 June 2008

Looking, learning and laughing with Bruce Goff

By Charles Jencks

In the run-up to a Tate Modern symposium this month, Charles Jencks looks at why US architect Bruce Goff is worth reassessing

Struckus House, California, 1979, from Heinz Emigholz’s film Goff in the Desert


It is impossible not to smile and laugh appreciatively when one visits a good Goff house, smile at the bad jokes and chuckle with an inward appreciation that Bruce Alonso Goff (1904-82) often gets the better of those architects who are invoked to justify his work. These paragons used to be Frank Lloyd Wright and Andrea Palladio, but today, in the Age of the Iconic Arms Race (as it is called), the names are Frank Gehry and Zaha Hadid.


Just as Marcel Duchamp made the art world revalue the readymade and reconsider pop and conceptual art, so has Bruce Goff forced us to reconsider the preoccupations that exercise today’s architecture.

A two-day seminar and screening of new films at the Tate Modern this month will illuminate the lessons this crafty celebrant has to teach, and let’s not take his programme too solemnly. Without laughter, his Mid-West send-ups lose half their point.


His most obvious lesson is in symphonic ornament, or using structure, materials and geometry in a musical way. Contemporary acronyms at opposite end of the spectrum, FOA and Fat, are advocating ornament for the ubiquitous blank surfaces that come with today’s commissions, and are producing more complex patterns than usual by exploiting computer production. That is exemplary, but they have yet to absorb Goff’s symbolic use of ornament around the ritual areas of a building — for instance the fireplace — or the formal culminations of the underlying design idea — for instance, the edges and crown of a building. It is these last formal rhythms, evident in Goff’s Price House and Studio, that are most significant in understanding the symphonic development of ornament: how it relates to the counterpoint of themes, the build-up to a climax or the diminution to a coda. If architecture is frozen music, then ornament really should orchestrate the basic ideas with development, transformation and contrast, not bang on repetitively, dumb-de-dumb-de-dumb. The plan of the Joe Price studio shows these variations on a theme, and Goff’s second main lesson.

Like virtually all the fashionable architects today, and particularly those showing at the fashion and architecture exhibit Skin and Bones at Somerset House, Goff was interested in the patterns of nature as an inspiration for architecture. Often it was a literal patterning of nature’s materiality, using black rubble masonry, black coal and blue glass cullet (all throwaway materials) to create amusing contrasts that can even the time: as the sun shines through the discarded crystal, it changes hue — “can you give me the colour, please?” Too often today, the patterns of nature are self-consciously displayed like a Boy Scout badge of eco-correctness, as if nature itself were always beautiful or good or interesting (not often ugly, vicious and kitsch).


Goff never falls for this sentimentality. His patterns both perform important roles in the overall symphonic order and reveal an appreciation for the underlying truth of nature: its geometry, as we now know, is mostly fractal. It varies the theme in self-similar patterns, ones that do not repeat exactly like a boring curtain wall.


Again, the plan of the Price studio shows this appreciation: the triangles, hexagons, lozenges and trihexes show a self-similarity of angle and a scaling from the very small detail to the shape of the wings to the scale of the whole. Fractals varying the theme are everywhere in his work, just as they are in a tree, cloud or river delta. As the Bavinger House plan shows, he even used a curved fractal form, the “strange attractor” of the spiral, as an organising shape.

This house, built in 1950 by the Bavingers themselves in Norman, Oklahoma, brings up another lesson: Goff’s witty use of technology and ready-made elements out of context. Here, steel cables are borrowed from biplane braces to support the spiral of the house. Lighting fixtures are made from an old gunner’s bubble robbed from a B52 bomber (“bomber blisters” — fantastic ad hocism!). Yankee ingenuity is combined with surrealist juxtaposition in a knowing way.


Anyone who doubts that Goff wanted to gently upstage Duchamp and Dali should study the Bavinger episode — it is instructive. The family borrowed everything they could get their hands on — fish nets for balustrades, central mast from the oil-drilling industry, cast-off rocks from the fields, uprooted walnut trees from neighbours — and charged the amazed and delighted locals $1 to watch construction, making $50,000 over the years for this performance art, which paid for the building. The beauty, freshness and humour of the house, not to mention its functionality and fractal cogency, are typical of Goff’s best work, and taken together these 30 or so houses should place him at the forefront of contemporary architecture, just as posthumously Duchamp has been in the art world.

It hasn’t yet happened, and for various reasons. For one thing, all of Goff’s best work is created with the client such as the Prices and Bavingers, and when that client is not on the job helping to get the detail, furnishings and ornament right, the result is unfortunate, as with the addition to the Los Angeles County Museum completed after Goff died in 1982. Architecture as an art today is rare, because of the productive and legal systems which more or less outlaw it. Goff never had much opportunity for large public commissions because here, especially, clients are unwilling to fulfil their historic role and take risks.

Inspiring affinities

When we invited Goff to speak at the Architectural Association in the 1970s, guessing that he might have something fresh to say, we asked him to speak on Antonio Gaudí’s work. The affinities proved inspiring, as Goff unearthed details and structural solutions in the Catalan that no historian had ever bothered to notice. Comment that, perhaps, only one great architect can make about a sympathetic predecessor. At the end, Goff received a standing ovation for several minutes, the only one in this sophisticated hotbed that I have witnessed over 40 years: the urbanites’ fulsome appreciation for their country cousin, the first Daniel Boone of architecture.

Now, 30 years on, Goff’s work fully deserves this further exposure.
